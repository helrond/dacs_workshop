What is DACS?

So, what is DACS?

A data content standard

Iterative

Accommodates single and multi level descriptions

I'm glad you asked! DACS is all of these things. We'll go over them one by one.

A way to consistently implement archival control

DACS is a way to consistently implement archival control.

Archival Control

DACS exists to...ensure the creation of consistent, appropriate, and self-explanatory descriptions of archival material

Introduction to Describing Archival Materials

In fact, this idea is written into DACS explicitly. But what does this mean? What is archival description?

What is Archival Description?

An approach to creating and managing information

Describing groupings of non-homogenous, non-self-describing materials

Requires abstraction, description of context of creation including creator

Describes not just materials, but people and their relationship to records (functions) as well

User-centric

Can be applied to materials regardless of format

What do we mean when we say "archival description?" We are fundamentally talking about an approach to creating and managing information, not a particular kind of output! This approach means we describe groups of unique things that are not self-describing (ie they don't have title pages, etc).
Because of this, we have to work differently than other information professionals; our description necessarily involves some abstraction as well as description of context.
It also means we have to constantly think of our users, which means we have to know who they are.
It's also important to note that even though we are usually describing unique materials, archival description can be applied to materials regardless of format or origin.
Finally, archival description is not just about the stuff, it's about describing people and their relationship to records as well!

A data content standard

DACS is also a data content standard. What is a data content standard?

Descriptive Standards

Rules and specifications that prescribe methods of producing uniform and consistent results or products for use in providing access to primary source materials.

Let's take a step back and talk about descriptive standards. There's a handy definition here on the screen. What I want us to focus on is the goal of all of this, which is access (not awesome description, even though that's a nice goal too). There are basically three kinds of descriptive standards.

Data structure standards

  • Define the elements of information contained in the components of an information system

    • Data structure standards define how data is structured; they tell you what fields exist and how they relate to each other.

Data content standards

  • Provide the rules to apply when recording information within each element defined in the data structure standards

    • Data content standards tell you what can go in the fields that are defined by data structure standards.

Data value standards

  • Provide lists or tables of terms, names, or other specific entities that are acceptable for entry in a particular data element

    • And data value standards, often considered a subset of data content standards, provide controlled lists of terms.

    • A companion standard

    DACS is a companion standard. What the heck is a companion standard? This means that DACS plays well with other standards like the ones we've just discussed. It is flexible enough to be used in conjunction with those standards.
    You might use rules from a more detailed, material-specific standard in lieu of DACS rules for specific elements if that form of material is particularly important to your repository.
    For example, rules for the Extent element from The IASA Cataloguing Rules: A Manual for the Description of Sound Recordings and Related Audiovisual Media provide much more detail for item-level descriptions of sound records than DACS does. If that matters in your repository, you can use them instead of the DACS Extent Element (2.5) rules.
    Or you might incorporate elements from other data content standards (e.g., not defined by ISAD(G), on which the DACS element set is based) in addition to DACS rules. For example, CCO provides elements (and rules for their content) for Style and for Culture that are beyond the scope of DACS and might be useful at the series- or item-levels to archivists describing cultural object components of the materials of an individual or organization

    So, this gives you a lot of flexibility, which is awesome. But it also gives you a lot of flexiblity, which is problematic. The point of standards is consistency, so in order to ensure you're still achieving that goal, you are going to need to create local implementation guidelines. So, make decisions, document those decisions, and the apply them consistently.
    Look to best practice guidelines (you’re not a special snowflake, someone else has probably encountered the same issue as you already!) developed by SAA. Check out some of the other SAA education offerings for more specific training.

    Based on and incorporates ISAD(G)

    In addition to being a companion standard, DACS has a closer relationship to the existing international standard for archival description. ISAD(G) is intended to be used with national standards.
    Let's talk a bit more about ISAD(G).

    ISAD(G) and DACS

    Only five elements are not totally parallel

    • Name and Location of Repository
    • Level of Description
    • Physical/Technical Access
    • Archival History/Custodial History
    • Archivist’s Note/Rules or Conventions/Date(s) of Description/Description Control

    DACS and ISAD(G) are really similar. This is because DACS is based on ISAD(G). As we just said, ISAD(G) is intended to be used in conjunction with national standards, which is why DACS has retained a very close connection to it.
    There are just a few differences:
    Name/Location of repository: ISAD(G) handles in reference code, CUSTARD group felt need for this in human-readable form.
    Level of description: Would look odd in finding aids if a separate element, and some data structures (EAD, for ex.) handle it. CUSTARD group decided to incorporate it as part of the Chapter 1 multi-level minimums
    Physical/Technical access: both there, just separately, in DACS.
    Archival history: CUSTARD group thought the name was weird – so renamed it but its content mirrors ISAD(G).
    3 elements to capture what archivists do in creating descriptions?: really, do we have any evidence that this is all that important to end users? One element seemed like enough for this, and the DACS rules capture the spirit of all 3 ISAD(G) elements.

    Why this matters

    Archival description is increasingly viewed and shared globally.

    Shared standards help archivists make description more widely available, and allow researchers to understand more easily.

    We need to keep in step with international standards because we're not on our own. That's why DACS is explicitly tied to ISAD(G).

    Output neutral

    DACS is output neutral. This means...

    Output neutrality

    Because it is designed to be used with other standards, DACS does not prescribe one specific kind of output.

    DACS can be used to create finding aids, catalog records, digital object metadata records, etc.

    Allows for data reuse: create description once, output in many ways.

    BUT, it's still all archival description!

    You can use DACS rules to create lots of different descriptive outputs. Not just finding aids. However, it's still all archival description because, as we just talked about, archival description is a way of managing information, not a specific kind of output about a specific kind of material.

    Iterative

    DACS is iterative. Which means...

    Iteration

    DACS can and should be used in almost every part of the archival enterprise, including accessioning, arrangement, description, reference.

    Archival processes are fundamentally iterative and involve accretion of knowledge, description, use. DACS accommodates this characteristic of archival functions and materials.

    Both that it can be used in almost every part of the archival enterprise, but also that it can be used whether you're doing extremely detailed item-level description, or just minimal collection-level description.

    Accommodates single and multi level descriptions

    DACS also accomodates single and multi-level descriptions. This is a bit tricky, but basically it means DACS allows you to describe collections at varying levels of granularity. Which means you can describe different levels of material (ie collection, series, item) at differing levels of detail. So, you can describe anything from a single item to an entire collection, and you can describe them in as much or as little detail as you want (once you’ve achieved a certain baseline). Note that these differing level of records can be created in any output desired.

    Navigating DACS

    DACS can be a little confusing. Here are some tips to help you navigate the standard.

    Sections

    DACS is divided into three sections. Part I, Part II and Appendices

    Part I - Describing Archival Materials

    • Divided into eight sections

    Part II - Archival Authority Records

    • Divided into six sections

    Appendices

    • Preface from 2004 edition
    • Companion Standards
    • Crosswalks

    Numbering

    Sections are numbered consecutively

    Rules within each section are also numbered consecutively

    Makes for easy citation/crosswalking

    Everything is numbered, which makes citing a particular rule very easy, and also makes you sound like you really know what you're talking about if you drop one of the numbers over a cocktail with other archivists.

    Requiredness

    “Explicitly state” or “record”: core data content component

    “If desirable” or “optionally”: rule that may or may not be used

    “Alternatively”: indicates a parallel approach

    DACS also uses some standard language when talking about requiredness.

    Common sections

    There are also some commmon sections that you'll find in many elements. Knowing what kinds of things you might expect to find in each can help you find a particular piece of information more easily.

    General Rules

    • Core rules governing an element

    Exclusions

    • Tell you what information shouldn’t go in a particular element
    • Clarify points of potential confusion

    Commentary

    • Not rules, but can help clarify terminology or explain the logic behind a rule

    Principles of DACS

    Let's talk about the principles of DACS.

    Principles of DACS

    On the nature of archival holdings

    On the relationship between arrangement and description

    On the nature of archival description

    On creators of archival material

    You should have looked at these already, but note that these principles are divided up into four conceptual groupings. These principles are important, and we're going to spend a bit of time going over them. You might think that a lot of these are common-sense, and they are, but that doesn't make them any less important.

    On the nature of archival holdings

    Principles 1 and 2

    Archival materials have unique characteristics

    These tell us what we already know; that archival materials are different than other kinds of material, and that it's important to pass information about those characteristics on to our researchers.

    • Aggregations of material
    • Created organically through course of business operations or person’s life
    • Intentionally accumulated through interest in some common characteristic such as topic or form of material

    How these materials were created, maintained and used is important to pass on to future users of the records (respect des fonds = provenance + original order)

    On the relationship between arrangement and description

    Principles 3 and 4

    Arrangement: identifying logical groupings as used and maintained by the creator of the aggregation, or constructing a useful organization if no original one is extant.

    Description: translating that arrangement and other important details about the aggregation into an access/use tool useful to both archivists and end users

    Tells us that arrangement and description are related; we also knew this already.

    On the nature of archival description

    Principles 5, 6 and 7

    We've talked about a lot of this already when talking about what DACS is. These principles are talking about single and multilevel description as well as output neutrality.

    Principles of archival description apply equally to all records regardless of how and who created them, and regardless of form or medium.

    Archival descriptions can be presented at varying levels of detail in a variety of outputs.

    Levels of description correspond to levels of arrangement; relationships between levels must be clearly identified; information provided at each level of description should be appropriate for that level.

    On creators of archival material

    Principle 8

    Archival description is about describing more than just materials. Describing creators and their relationship to the materials is crucial to understanding those materials.

    We know this too, but it's important to say; this is basically the reason that Part II exists.
    Those are the principles. Pause for questions.

    Development of DACS

    I think it's important for us to know where DACS came from; it helps explain some of the decisions that were made. And, as archivists, we should love context, right?

    How did we get here?

    Anglo-American Cataloguing Rules (AACR2): 1967

    AACR2 (1967) Rules for general and media-specific cataloging, Chapter 4 addressed specifically to cataloging manuscripts.

    Archives, Personal Papers and Manuscripts (APPM): 1983

    AACR2 was seen as too limited. So APPM was developed as an adaptation of AACR2 Chapter 4 to cover materials other than just manuscripts. It is applicable to all media, though guidance is minimal for non-text. However, it is for cataloging only; does not address finding aids or other methods of description. Specifically addresses modern manuscripts,

    General International Standard Archival Description (ISAD(G)): 1994

    So along came ISAD(G), which is the international standard for description of archival materials. We've talked a bit about this already, but this standard was developed by the International Council on Archives.

    International Standard Archival Authority Record For Corporate Bodies, Persons and Families (ISAAR(CPF)): 1996

    This same group also developed an international standard for creating authority records for people, organizations and families.

    Rules for Archival Description (RAD): 1990

    At the same time, in a land far away, Canadians were developing their own rules for archival description. Canadian rules for archival description.

    Canadian-United States Task Force on Archival Description (CUSTARD): 2001-2003

    With the creation of ISAD(G) and ISAAR(CPF), a joint US/Canadian task force was formed to attempt to reconcile RAD, APPM and ISAD(G) to create common standard for US/Canadian archival description. However, this didn't work out, and revealed significant divergences in approach/practice between US and Canadian archivists.

    Describing Archives: A Content Standard (DACS): 2004

    DACS (2004) Came out of CUSTARD process

    What's new?

    Minimum, Optimum, Added Value indicated with each element

    Encoding examples not embedded in text

    Have been separated to website; examples often become the rule

    “Devised” used to indicate titles created by an archivist

    As opposed to "supplied"

    Acronyms should be spelled out at least once

    Abbreviations and symbols discouraged

    These are more explicitly spelled out than in the previous edition

    “Papers”, “records”, “collection” not prescribed for titles

    This is a recognition that we are in an era where our collections are no longer just analog materials.

    Additional rules for Processing note

    Information about maintenance/reconstitution of original order, arrangement by the archivist, or previous arrangements or reorganization(s) by the creator if known and important to the understanding of the materials should be recorded in Processing Note.

    Part II substantially revised, Part III eliminated

    Part II covers Archival Authority Records, Identifying Creators and Administrative/Biographical History.

    Appendices and Crosswalks revised

    Number of crosswalks in Appendix C have been reduced. Appendix D removed in favor of expanded examples on the companion website

    Introduction to Describing Archival Materials

    “Consistent, appropriate, and self-explanatory descriptions of archival material”

    Start by describing generally, move to the specific

    Let's take a look at the Introduction first. The important things to note here: there is an up-front reference to professional judgement. This does not mean anything goes. It means you *can* (and must) make certain decisions, but those decisions must then be documented and applied consistently.
    We describe starting at the general and moving to the specific. We start with information that applies to *everything* in the archival unit being described, and then provide more detailed information specific to certain pieces of that unit as necessary.

    Identity Elements (Section 2)

    We already talked about Section 1, which is levels of description.

    2.1 Reference Code

    When you see a checkmark next to the element title, it's a require element.

    Uniquely identifies the archival unit being described

    May consist of three subelements

    • a local identifier
    • a code for the repository
    • and a code for the country

    Codes for country and repository should come from data value standards

    In the US, this would be MARC Code list for organizations

    Repositories should develop system for identifying units locally

    This often takes the form of some letters followed by sequential numbers

    2.2 Name and Location of Repository

    Pretty self-explanatory, but also important: fundamental to in-person access!

    Think about what's important for researchers to know.
    Good example of the DACS approach to “requiredness” in the rules

    2.3 Title

    A word (infrequently) or a phrase (more likely) by which the archival unit can be identified.

    Sometimes material will have formal titles (like a book or an essay).

    Most titles will have to be devised by archivists. There are no rules for transcribing formal titles; look to another standard.

    Titles should...

    Be brief: use other elements for lengthy text

    Don't make the title do all the work! Title needs to work as citation.

    Not use acronyms, abbreviations or symbols

    Many archivists like to use brackets, but DACS does not support this practice for the simple reason that they have potentially many different meanings. Don't make your researchers have to research your titles.

    Have a name segment

    Name segment: person, corporate body or family primarily responsible for creating or assembling the materials. Rules that cover multiple creators, name changes, etc.

    Have a segment describing the nature of the archival unit

    Nature of Archival Unit segment: Generally, papers, records and collections. However, other names may be used. As always, document what you decide to do, and then do it consistently.

    Have a segment describing the topics covered in the archival

    Topics segment: Optional, May be useful when name and form segments don’t give users enough information, or are misleading on their own.

    2.4 Date

    There are lots of different kinds of dates

    There is no default kind of date to record (2.4.3), but we most often record dates of creation. This will vary by repository's needs and material being described.

    Clearly label what kind it is

    Rules for date ranges, predominant or bulk dates

    Don’t use abbreviations such as ca., n.d.

    You'll hear this over and over again; don't make your researchers guess!

    2.5 Extent

    Can be described either in terms of physical extent (number of boxes, linear feet) or material types.

    In each case, record the numerical quantity associated with the extent or material type

    Two parts to this element.

    Lots of rules for different ways to do multiple, approximate, parallel statements.

    Rules for electronic records.

    This is an element that you really need to establish local practice for. Can be critical for repository management and reporting.

    Content and Structure Elements (Section 3)

    3.1 Scope and Content

    Provides information about the nature of the materials and activities reflected in the unit being described

    Enable users to judge relevance

    This is very important to remember!

    May include information about any or all of the following:

    • the function(s), activity(ies), transaction(s), and process(es) that generated the materials
    • the documentary form(s) or intellectual characteristics of the records
    • the time period(s) covered by the intellectual content or subject of the materials
    • geographic area(s) and places to which the records pertain
    • subject matter to which the records pertain, such as topics, events, people, and organizations
    • any other information that assists the user in evaluating the relevance of the materials, such as completeness, changes in location, ownership and custody while still in the possession of the creator, etc

    3.2 System of Arrangement

    Describes three things

    Describes current arrangement of materials

    Describes relationships between groupings of materials

    DACS calls this the whole-part relationship/

    Describes ordering system for component files or items (if any)

    Don’t record information about other actions taken by archivists that affected arrangement here; put it in a processing note.

    Conditions of Access and Use elements (Section 4)

    4.1 Conditions Governing Access

    Restrictions due to the nature of the information in the materials being described, such as those imposed by the donor, by the repository, or by statutory/regulatory requirements, including:

    • Extent of materials restricted
    • Authority for restriction
    • Duration of restriction
    • Authorized users
    • Contact person for further information

    If no restrictions exist, you should say so.

    Be clear and brief. Establish a set of standardized statements.

    Often confused with Conditions Governing Use element.

    4.2 Physical Access

    Restrictions due to any physical characteristics or storage locations that limit, restrict, delay, or otherwise affect access to the materials being described, including:

    • Location

      • If this location affects (ie delays or limits) use: offsite, cold storage

    • Physical condition of the material

      • Again, if it limits use

    • Requirement to use copies instead of originals for preservation reasons (not merely the fact of their existence)

    4.3 Technical Access

    Restrictions due to any technical requirements that restrict or otherwise affect access to the materials being described, such as:

    • Special projection or playback equipment
    • Specific hardware/software required for use
    • Particularly relevant for electronic records

    This is an element that will likely be used more in the future as digital materials become more common in archives.

    4.4 Conditions Governing Reproduction and Use

    Restrictions on reproduction due to copyright or other reasons

    Restrictions on further use of the materials being described, such as publication, after access has been provided

    If no restrictions exist, you should say so

    Again, use /= access. Standardized language very useful here

    4.5 Languages and Scripts of the Material

    Identifies languages or scripts in use

    If there are none (for example, it’s a collection of photographs), say “no linguistic content”

    Note that this is a required element; often forgotten.

    4.6 Finding Aids

    Identifies any other finding aids to the materials being described

    These don’t necessarily have to be DACS-compliant finding aids; any tools that help researchers understand materials.

    Can be things like indexes, card files, record schedules, etc.

    Acquisition and Appraisal Elements (Section 5)

    5.1 Custodial History

    Information on changes of ownership or custody of the material being described, from the time it left the possession of the creator until it was acquired by the repository

    Should be recorded if this information is critical to authenticity, integrity, and interpretation of archival unit

    5.2 Immediate Source of Acquisition

    Source of acquisition and relationship to materials

    Means of acquisition

    Date of acquisition

    Other information if relevant

    Brief statement. Custodial history is where a more extended account should go.

    5.3 Appraisal, Destruction, and Scheduling Information

    What happened, who did it, when it was done

    Rationale for appraisal, destruction actions

    Relevant disposition schedules

    Do not record information about gaps in records for reasons other than archival appraisal or destruction

    5.4 Accruals

    Information about anticipated accruals frequency and volume

    Related Materials Elements (Section 6)

    6.1 Existence and Location of Originals

    Should only be used if you are describing material that is NOT the original, like microfilm copies or digital surrogates

    Record where the materials are available and how to access them

    6.2 Existence and Location of Copies

    Indicates the existence, location, and availability of copies or other reproductions of the materials being described

    Excludes copies not publicly available

    Excludes copies made for preservation reasons

    If copies MUST be used, record that information in Physical Access element

    6.3 Related Archival Materials

    Existence and location of archival materials that are closely related to the materials

    Relationship can be based on provenance, functions, or subject matter, but it should be direct and significant

    Don’t make this element do the work of a discovery system; this is not the place for a subject guide. Think about how you are going to keep this information current. You can also use access points and authority records to do this.

    6.4 Publication Note

    Record publications that are about or are based on the use, study, or analysis of the materials being described

    Not publications you consulted in writing the finding aid

    7 Note elements

    Information not accommodated by any of the other elements

    • Conservation actions, including electronic records (migration, reformatting)
    • Preferred citation
    • Identifiers
    • Variant Title
    • Processing Information

    Should be used thoughtfully. Will this information help the end user? Not a place for collection management or internal documentation.

    8 Description Control elements

    Document who created the description and how they created it

    • Sources used
    • Rules/conventions used
    • Name of person who created it
    • Dates of creation and revision

    Profession has shown increased emphasis on exposing the role of the archivist. Own the postmodernity.

    Overview of Elements: Part II

    Authority Records

    What is an authority record and what does it do?

    Identifies and describes person, corporate body or family associated with archival materials

    Documents relationships between these entities and archival materials

    May control form of name locally

    Relies on consistency and uniqueness

    This part of DACS has been revised and expanded extensively. This is largely due to the approval of EAC-CPF as an SAA standard. So let's talk a bit about what authority records are and what they do.

    Advantages of authority records

    • Reduce duplication (and potential for errors)
    • Linking of one-to-many and many-to-one
    • Single point of access for physically disparate materials
    • Reduce maintenance
    • More structured description of record-creation context
    • Can help identify complex entities (name changes, etc)
    • Facilitates data exchange

    As you can see, there are a lot of advantages to using authority records. That said, not every institution will have the ability or desire to create authority records.

    What do they contain?

    • Standardized form of name, variant names (identity)
    • History and activities of the entity being described (description)
    • Relationships to other entities and information resources (relationships)
    • Documentation of how the record was created and maintained (control)

    Traditionally, archivists have presented information about creators within the context of description of materials. There’s nothing wrong with that. This part of DACS provides guidance for institutions that have a separate system for authority data (and that doesn’t necessarily mean EAC-CPF!) In general, you will find that the rules in this section are much more detailed, and often point you to data value standards as sources of information.

    Minimum elements

    10.1 Authorized Form of the Name

    10.2 Type of Entity

    11.1 Dates of Existence

    13.2 Authority Record Identifier

    Similar philosophy to archival materials. Only four minimum elements.

    Added value elements

    Anything else you want to add!

    This does not mean a free-for-all. Again, you should make decisions, document them, and then apply them consistently.

    10 Form of the Name

    10.1 Authorized Form of the Name

    Should come from a standardized vocabulary (data value standard)

    10.2 Type of Entity

    What kind of entity is this? Doesn’t necessarily have to be human-readable

    10.3 Variant Names

    Rules for recording variant names. These are slightly different from library rules, so if you decide to deviate from DACS here, make sure you document it and do it consistently. Key here is to add names that will help users find materials (“might reasonably be sought”)

    10.4 Identifiers for Corporate Bodies

    Record an identifier if the entity is a corporate body. This should come from a data value standard

    11 Description of the Person, Family, or Corporate Body

    11.1 Dates of Existence

    Detailed guidance on how to record various kinds of dates. Referenced throughout the rest of Part II.

    11.2 Historical Summary

    Similar to the biographical/historical element; a narrative note

    11.3 Places

    Record place (from data value standard) and dates

    11.4 Legal Status

    For corporate bodies only; use controlled vocabulary and record dates

    11.5 Functions, Occupations, Activities

    Terms should come from a controlled vocabulary if at all possible. Not a narrative note!

    11.6 Mandates/Source of Authority

    A law, act or other legal directive on which the authority of a corporate body rests. NOT a source you consulted to create the record!

    11.7 Internal Structure/Genealogy

    Narrative note documenting internal structure (or genealogy) of the entity being described, significant changes to this structure, and relationships within this structure

    12 Related Persons, Families, and Corporate Bodies

    Many kinds of relationships - family, successor/predecessor, departments, officers, etc. NOT related archival material - that has it’s own elements

    12.1. Names/Identifiers of Related Corporate Bodies, Persons, or Families

    Take the name from a data value standard

    12.2. Type of Related Entity Record

    What kind of entity this related entity is

    12.3 Description of the Relationship

    Two rules, both of which require you to use a controlled vocabulary. Allows you to decide how detailed you want to be about describing this relationship, but also establishes a baseline for information

    12.4 Dates of the Relationship

    Record the dates of the relationship

    13 Authority Record Management

    13.1. Repository Code

    Should be from a national controlled vocabulary (which is LC in the US)

    13.2 Authority Record Identifier

    Gives you the option to have a local system to uniquely identify each record

    13.3 Rules or Conventions

    Cite rules you used to create the record (will likely include more than just DACS). Specify rules for form of name separately. Note directive to establish local practice.

    13.4 Status

    Allows you to say whether or not the record is current, obsolete, under revision, etc

    13.5 Level of Detail

    DACS defines minimal You’ll need to define what “partial” or “full” mean

    13.6 Date(s) of Authority Record Creation and Revision

    dates of record (not entity)

    13.7 Languages or Scripts

    language of record (not materials)

    13.8 Sources

    Sources consulted in preparing the record. Establish local practice regarding form of citation.

    13.9 Maintenance Information

    name of person who created or revised the record other information about its creation or maintenance

    14 Related Archival Materials and Other Resources

    Does not specify mechanics of relationships, just information that should be recorded. How you link things (or not) is up to you.

    14.1 Identifiers and Titles of Related Resources

    Information needed to uniquely identify an information resource

    14.2 Types of Related Resources

    Record the type of resource. Again, use a controlled vocabulary.

    14.3 Nature of Relationship to Related Resources

    Record nature of relationship: creator, author, custodian, owner, etc.

    14.4 Dates of Related Resources and/or Relationships

    Dates of resource Dates related to relationship Describe what those dates are
